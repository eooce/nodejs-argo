The curved stroke that encloses a counter. Found in b, d, g, p, q, R.

The enclosed or partially enclosed space within a letter. What isn't drawn matters as much as what is.

06 — History
From chisel to pixel
c. 1440

Gutenberg's Movable Type

Johannes Gutenberg cast individual metal letters that could be arranged, inked, and pressed onto paper. The printing press democratized knowledge and gave birth to typographic craft as we know it.

c. 1470

Jenson's Roman Type

Nicolas Jenson cut a typeface in Venice that abandoned the heavy Gothic blackletter for lighter, more open roman forms. It became the model for centuries of serif design.

1757

Baskerville's Precision

John Baskerville pushed type into sharper contrast between thick and thin strokes, using glossy paper and heated copper plates. His work bridged old-style and modern type.

1896

Akzidenz-Grotesk

Berthold's sans-serif typeface stripped away serifs with industrial efficiency. It predated Helvetica by decades and established the visual language of modernism.

1957

Helvetica

Max Miedinger designed Neue Haas Grotesk, later renamed Helvetica. It became the most ubiquitous typeface on Earth — from subway signs to corporate logos to tax forms.

1996

CSS Font Control

The CSS1 specification gave web designers basic font-family, size, and weight control. Typography left the page and entered the browser — though with only a handful of "web-safe" fonts.

2010

Google Fonts Launch

Free, open-source web fonts exploded onto the internet. Suddenly, anyone could use professionally designed typefaces — 900+ families and counting — without licensing friction.

2020s

Variable Fonts

A single font file containing an entire design space — weight, width, optical size all on continuous axes. The most significant technical leap since OpenType, enabling responsive typography.
